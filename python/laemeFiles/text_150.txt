{=Script - proto-Gothic book hand with typical C13 forking of ascenders.\n
It has some similarities to contemporary documentary hands (cf. e.g. Johnson and\n
Jenkinson (1915 [1967], Plate 16). The form of wynn made with three\n
strokes  and with an open top is unusual. See Wright (1960 no. 8)=}\n
{=Status - MS punctuation done; tagging notes and textual notes up to date=}\n
{=Verse is written as prose. Punctuation mostly confined to a punctus at\n
the end of every verse line. The Latin titles are evidently in red.\n
Most of the letters at the beginning of verse lines appear from the\n
microfilm to have been rubricated to make them litterae notabiliores.\n
As with the verse texts on fol. 3r-v, I have only marked with an\n
asterisk those that have majuscule form. In many places the final text\n
has been written over an erasure. It is very difficult to tell from black and\n
white microfilm where all of these occur. I have noted any that are\n
clear and especially when the rewriting does not fit the space left.\n
For other notices of erasures see Hall (1920 no. XXI. The scribe, both\n
here and in the verses on fol. 3r-v, uses two distinct types of <g>\n
with distinct functions. Both have a single lobe and a leftward\n
curving tail as in a normal Caroline \'g\'. One has an off stroke\n
linking it with the following littera - this \'hooked\' <g> stands\n
for [g] and the rare occurrences of [d3]. The other lacks the off\n
stroke or \'hook\' and stands for all other functions of \'g\'. See\n
Wirtjes (1991: x) and the reference there to Gumbert and Vermeer\n
(1971). Gumbert and Vermeer refer to the hookless <g> as \'an unusual\n
yogh\' but its shape is nothing like yogh being identical to the\n
scribe\'s normal <g> but simply lacking the offstroke and here I\n
recognise this \'g\' as G2=}\n
