{=English text follows a verse text in French in the same hand which\n
ends at line 8 of the recto leaf. Some decorative swirls divide it from\n
the Poema Morale. As with the other Egerton version of PM, The initial\n
letters of each couplet were presumably intended to be capitals and\n
placed within separate ruled lines. On the first recto, only the first\n
large initial is coloured and the initial letters of all the\n
subsequent lines are capital in form and placed within the special\n
ruling, following the scribe\'s practice in the preceding French text.\n
After the first recto the alternate lines begin with minuscule figurae\n
and are indented. The initials of the first line of each couplet are\n
not always capitals in shape but are obviously coloured, as they are\n
very faint on microfilm, and sometimes seem to be absent altogether.\n
Those I can make out with confidence are recorded and are marked with\n
an asterisk as litterae notabiliores. Punctuation is mostly confined\n
to punctus midline. This scribe has a very unusual strategy on the\n
use of accents over vowels. My usual transcription policy is to\n
ignore oblique strokes or dots on I, J (i-longa) or Y as being an\n
integral part of the figura of those litterae. In the case of I/J\n
dots or oblique strokes are commonly used (as precursors to the modern\n
dot) presumably to differentiate the vowel from surrounding minims\n
implying consonantal letters. With Y the intention is perhaps to\n
differentiate the littera from either wynn or, in northerly texts,\n
from consonantal Y for thorn, though these practices are rarely\n
followed consistently. Oblique strokes over vowels other than these\n
are taken to be not integral to the figurae and these accents, which\n
are for the most part explicable as length markers, are indicated\n
with a lower-case x following the vowel. This scribe has a habit of\n
placing accents over vowels much more frequently than any other\n
scribe I have come across. Because the usage is unusual and because\n
accents on the letter I seem not to be different in kind or frequency\n
from those over other vowels I have, in this one text only, extended\n
the use of lower case x as a marker of accent also to its appearance\n
on I/J. Vocalic Y is regularly dotted not accented and I have ignored\n
the dot as being part of the figura. The accents seem not to be\n
confined to long vowels, or to be at all regular, but certain patterns\n
are observable. Accents frequently appear on final long vowels, e.g.\n
the E of the personal pronouns HE and wE and the I of HI; on DO\n
infinitive; also on prefixed A+ and I+, and in the words EFT(er),\n
OFT, OF, GOD "good", wAT "know" and wEL av. The significance, if any,\n
of these accents, is unclear to me=}\n
